  
Executive summary

Preface

DEWR currently provides all employees with access to Generative AI. All employees can access the free Microsoft Copilot Chat and just under 10% can access the paid M365 Copilot with greater integration capability. In March 2026, nearly 80% of all staff used one of these tools, entering over 250,000 prompts, or about 4 prompts per person every workday.

In January 2026, DEWR ran a trial in which 5% of employees were provided access to Public Generative AI tools, in addition to their existing copilot access, for a period of x weeks. The trial cohort was selected at random and stratified to ensure representation across Groups, across Copilot Chat and M365 Copilot users, and across APS levels. The tools were the free versions of Open AI’s ChatGPT, Google’s Gemini, and Anthropic’s Claude, which could be accessed in web browsers. Staff were provided with both technical and governance instruction, including on what could be uploaded to the tools \[unclassified information only\]. Technical protections were established to reduce the likelihood of classified being uploaded to the tools.

At the conclusion of the trial period, all participants were invited to complete a voluntary survey about their experience using Microsoft Copilot and the Public Generative AI tools. The trial was intended to assess:

• the current productivity of Copilot (both versions)  
• whether the Public Generative AI tools provided additional value beyond Copilot  
• the relative utility of each of the selected PGAI tools  
• the potential productivity benefits from the PGAI tools  
• the degree of concern staff have when using the tools

Approximately half of all trial participants responded to the survey (52%), with x% indicating that they had actively participated in the trial. The sample is sufficient…

Insights from the evaluation

Copilot usage and productivity

This section examines the productivity value DEWR is already getting from Copilot. It compares M365 Copilot and Copilot Chat users to understand whether access type affects how often staff use Copilot, what they use it for, how useful they find it, and how much time it saves.

M365 Copilot users reported materially higher time savings. Average reported time saved was 69 minutes per day for M365 Copilot users, compared with 34 minutes for Copilot Chat/basic users. For a full-time employee, this equates to approximately 5.7 hours per week for M365 Copilot and 2.8 hours per week for Copilot Chat.

2\. Public Gen AI uptake, use and productivity

Public Gen AI tools improved productivity for many users, however value was not evenly distributed.

• 80% of valid users rated at least one public tool as moderately useful or better.  
• 72% said free public tools would provide value over and above Copilot.  
• 72% agreed or strongly agreed that they wanted to continue using at least one public tool.  
• 53% used public tools at least a few times a week during the trial.

Prior Gen AI experience was the clearest observed indicator of stronger outcomes, with experienced and highly experienced users reporting higher regular use, usefulness and continuation intent. Value also varies by APS/EL level, Copilot access type, and organisational group. These patterns suggest user capability, role context, and the nature of work shaped how much benefit staff drew from public Gen AI tools.

2.2 ChatGPT had the broadest uptake, while Claude showed the strongest usefulness and continuation intent

ChatGPT had the broadest uptake, with 92% using ChatGPT. Claude was used by 67% of respondents and Gemini by 61%. This suggests ChatGPT was the most widely adopted public tool, but not necessarily the tool that users valued most strongly.

Usefulness was positive across all three tools. Most users of each tool rated it moderately useful or better: 62.5% for ChatGPT, 70.7% for Claude and 64.9% for Gemini. Claude also had the strongest high-usefulness result, with 46.3% of Claude users rating it very or extremely useful, compared with 28.6% for ChatGPT and 29.7% for Gemini.

Continuation intent followed a similar pattern. Claude had the strongest continuation signal, with 63% of valid respondents agreeing or strongly agreeing that they wanted to continue using it after the trial. ChatGPT also had a positive continuation signal, with 54% wanting to continue, while Gemini was lower at 43%.

2.4 Public tools offered productivity gains beyond Copilot, but value varied by access type

Compared with Copilot, public Gen AI tools were most positively rated for output quality. Claude had the strongest comparative profile across most productivity enablers. It performed well on output quality, time saved, work support and response speed. ChatGPT’s strongest relative result was prompt understanding, while Gemini was less likely than ChatGPT or Claude to be seen as delivering clear productivity gains over Copilot.

These comparisons also varied by Copilot access type. Public tools appeared to provide clearer marginal value for Copilot Chat/basic users than for M365 Copilot users, particularly for ChatGPT. Averaged across output quality, time saved, work support, speed and prompt understanding, 47% of Copilot Chat/basic users rated ChatGPT better than Copilot, compared with 27% of M365 Copilot users. Gemini showed a weaker and more even pattern, at 27% for Copilot Chat/basic users and 24% for M365 Copilot users. Claude was the main exception, with 44% of M365 Copilot users rating it better than Copilot, compared with 39% of Copilot Chat/basic users.

2.5: Lack of integration, free-tier limits and reliability issues prevented benefits from scaling evenly

The most common barrier was lack of integration with internal systems and Microsoft 365 products, reported by 49% of public-tool users. Public tools may produce useful outputs, but staff still need to manually copy, paste, adapt and validate material across systems. Free prompt or request limits were the next most common barrier, reported by 41% of respondents.

Reliability issues also limited the consistency of benefits. Misinterpreted prompts and difficulty with specialised topics were each reported by 34% of respondents, while slow response times were reported by 28%. Made up facts or fabricated content were less common, at 15%, but remain important because even a small number of unreliable outputs can reduce trust and increase the need for manual checking.

The limitation profile also varied by tool. Lack of integration was common across all three tools, affecting 48% of ChatGPT users, 49% of Gemini users and 51% of Claude users. Free prompt or request limits were more concentrated in ChatGPT, reported by 36% of ChatGPT users, compared with 20% of Claude users and 5% of Gemini users. Gemini had the highest share of users reporting difficulty with specialised topics, at 38%.

# **Public Gen AI Trial: Mitigation Effectiveness Analysis**

*Additional report section covering DLP, splash screens and training/guidance effectiveness*

Overall assessment: The trial mitigations were effective enough to support a controlled trial, but not sufficient as a standalone model for broader rollout. The strongest evidence is that safety communications and splash screens helped create informed caution. Around seven in ten valid users rated each as moderately or highly effective, users who rated both positively were more likely to feel comfortable using public tools, reported security concerns were rare, and no valid user rated upload blockers as ineffective. However, most users did not notice upload blockers and copy/paste behaviour was more common than file upload, meaning the main residual risk depends heavily on user judgement rather than technical blocking.

## **Scope and denominator**

This section uses the valid public-tool user population defined in the project data notes. Although 71 respondents said they participated in the Public Gen AI Trial, 10 did not later report using any of ChatGPT, Gemini or Claude. These 10 are excluded from the valid public-tool user population, leaving 61 valid users.

* Total survey responses: 104\.  
* Respondents who said they participated in the trial: 71\.  
* Valid public-tool users: 61\.  
* Excluded from the valid user population: 10 respondents who said they participated but did not report using ChatGPT, Gemini or Claude.  
* Percentages in this section use the valid public-tool user denominator unless stated otherwise. Denominators vary where questions were optional or skipped.

## **Headline findings**

* Introductory email: 45 of 61 valid users, or 73.8%, rated the email moderately or highly effective.  
* Splash screens: 43 of 60 valid users, or 71.7%, rated splash screens moderately or highly effective.  
* Combined communications: 40 of 60 valid users, or 66.7%, rated both the email and splash screens moderately or highly effective. 47 of 60, or 78.3%, rated at least one of the two communication channels moderately or highly effective.  
* Security confidence: 46 of 61 valid users, or 75.4%, were comfortable or very comfortable using public tools.  
* Reported concerns: 2 of 61 valid users, or 3.3%, reported a security concern. 7 of 61, or 11.5%, reported an ethical concern.  
* DLP/upload blockers: 10 of 60 valid users, or 16.7%, rated upload blockers effective. 50 of 60, or 83.3%, did not notice them. No valid user rated upload blockers as not effective.  
* Data handling behaviour: 26 of 61 valid users, or 42.6%, uploaded documents. 43 of 61, or 70.5%, copied and pasted information.

## **1\. Safety communications and splash screens were generally effective**

The introductory email and splash screens were both rated positively by most valid users. This matters because they served different purposes: the email provided up-front framing before use, while splash screens acted as point-of-use reminders when users entered public Gen AI websites.

*\[Figure missing: safety\_comms\_effectiveness.png\]*

The result supports a positive assessment of the communications layer. The introductory email and splash screens were not only noticed by active users, but were rated as moderately or highly effective by around seven in ten respondents. The splash screen result is especially important because it suggests the point-of-use reminder did not substantially underperform the introductory email.

## **2\. Safety communications were associated with higher security comfort**

The survey cannot prove that communications caused users to feel safer, but the pattern is consistent with communications supporting safer and more confident use.

* Among users who rated the introductory email moderately or highly effective, 36 of 45, or 80.0%, were comfortable or very comfortable. Among other users, 10 of 16, or 62.5%, were comfortable or very comfortable.  
* Among users who rated splash screens moderately or highly effective, 35 of 43, or 81.4%, were comfortable or very comfortable. Among other users, 11 of 18, or 61.1%, were comfortable or very comfortable.  
* Among users who rated both the email and splash screens moderately or highly effective, 33 of 40, or 82.5%, were comfortable or very comfortable. Among other users, 13 of 21, or 61.9%, were comfortable or very comfortable.

This is useful evidence for the report because it links mitigation exposure to an outcome. Positive ratings of the email and splash screens aligned with higher security confidence. The safest interpretation is that communications helped create informed caution and gave users more confidence about the trial boundaries.

## **3\. Most users were comfortable, and reported security concerns were rare**

*\[Figure missing: security\_confidence\_concerns.png\]*

Most valid users were comfortable operating under the trial guardrails. A quarter remained uncomfortable, which should not automatically be interpreted as a poor outcome. In a public-tool environment, the desired state is not necessarily complete comfort. A level of caution may be appropriate, especially where users are making judgement calls about what information can be shared.

Only 2 of 61 valid users reported a security concern. The open-text responses do not indicate a confirmed data-loss incident. One respondent described pausing before sending prompts because some project management artefacts were hard to classify. Another described discomfort with Gemini access or verification. This points to residual uncertainty about classification and user judgement, rather than a clear control failure.

Ethical concerns were more common than security concerns, but most were broader concerns about AI rather than specific trial incidents. These included environmental impact, model training data, misinformation, over-reliance, bias and loss of critical thinking. One specific output-related concern was raised where a respondent felt public tools produced less sensitive responses than Copilot on a sensitive policy topic. This supports retaining human review and APS-context guardrails for future use.

## **4\. DLP upload blockers appear to have operated as a backstop, but the survey gives limited direct evidence of technical effectiveness**

*\[Figure missing: dlp\_upload\_blockers.png\]*

The upload blocker result should be interpreted carefully. Most valid users did not notice upload blockers, but this is not the same as saying the blockers were ineffective. Upload blockers would only be visible to users if they attempted to upload material that triggered the control. The absence of any “Not Effective” ratings among valid users is reassuring, but it does not prove that DLP was tested frequently or comprehensively.

* Among all valid users with a response to the blocker item, 10 of 60, or 16.7%, rated upload blockers effective and 50 of 60, or 83.3%, did not notice them.  
* Among the 26 users who uploaded documents, 8, or 30.8%, rated upload blockers effective and 18, or 69.2%, did not notice them.  
* Among users who did not upload documents, 2 of 34 with a blocker response, or 5.9%, rated blockers effective and 32 of 34, or 94.1%, did not notice them.  
* Security concerns were rare among both document uploaders and non-uploaders: 1 of 26 among uploaders and 1 of 35 among non-uploaders.

This supports a cautious conclusion: DLP likely operated as a backstop, but the main mitigation observable in the survey was user caution reinforced by communications and trial rules. For a stronger assurance position, survey evidence should be supplemented with system logs showing attempted uploads, blocked uploads and classification triggers.

## **5\. Copy/paste behaviour is the bigger residual risk than file upload**

The survey suggests the main residual data-handling risk may be less about document upload and more about text transfer. This is important because upload blockers are better suited to controlling file movement than judgement-based decisions about text copied from emails, meeting notes, code, data or internal documents.

* 26 of 61 valid users, or 42.6%, uploaded documents.  
* 43 of 61 valid users, or 70.5%, copied and pasted information.  
* Security concerns among copy/paste users were still rare: 2 of 43 copy/paste users reported a security concern, compared with 0 of 18 users who did not copy/paste information.

This finding has practical implications for future rollout. DLP controls should remain in place, but future mitigations should focus just as strongly on safe prompting, copy/paste behaviour, and examples of information that should not be entered into public tools. Training should not only explain what files can be uploaded. It should also explain what text can be copied, pasted, summarised or transformed.

## **6\. Residual discomfort shows the mitigations created caution, not complete confidence**

Fifteen of the 61 valid users said they were uncomfortable using public tools. This group is analytically useful because it shows the limits of the mitigations and helps distinguish between informed caution and unresolved concern.

* Of the 15 uncomfortable valid users, 9 still rated the introductory email moderately or highly effective.  
* Of the 15 uncomfortable valid users, 8 still rated splash screens moderately or highly effective.  
* Of the 15 uncomfortable valid users, 4 uploaded documents.  
* Of the 15 uncomfortable valid users, 10 copied and pasted information.

This suggests some users remained cautious even when they understood the rules. That is not necessarily a negative outcome. For public Gen AI tools, appropriate caution is a form of risk mitigation. However, it also shows that communications alone did not eliminate uncertainty, especially for users dealing with borderline information or unclear classification markings.

## **7\. Non-user responses show where mitigations did not go far enough**

Non-user responses provide a useful counterpoint to the valid-user findings. Among the 33 respondents who said they did not use public tools, 8, or 24.2%, cited security or confidentiality concerns. A further 7, or 21.2%, cited uncertainty or skill barriers, meaning they were not sure what they could use the tools for or did not know how to use them.

The 10 respondents excluded from the valid public-tool user population are also informative. These respondents said they participated, but did not report using ChatGPT, Gemini or Claude. Among this group, 5 of 10 were uncomfortable using public tools. Only 3 of 9 rated the introductory email moderately or highly effective, and only 4 of 9 rated splash screens moderately or highly effective.

This suggests the mitigations were enough to support many active users, but not enough to bring all selected participants into confident and safe use. For future rollout, the department should consider more practical onboarding, examples of appropriate use, and clearer guidance for staff who are unsure whether their work is suitable for public tools.

## **8\. Training and capability uplift: evidence is positive but indirect**

The survey did not include a direct “training effectiveness” question. The best available proxy is whether users reported that their Gen AI experience improved during the trial. This provides evidence that the combination of trial access, communications, splash screens and hands-on use helped build capability.

* 54 of 61 valid users, or 88.5%, said their Gen AI experience improved at least a little.  
* 35 of 61 valid users, or 57.4%, said their Gen AI experience improved moderately, a lot or significantly.

This supports the conclusion that the trial had a capability-building effect. However, capability uplift should not be treated as evidence that all users understood risk settings. The non-user results and qualitative feedback show that some staff still needed clearer examples of what information could be shared, how to classify ambiguous material, and how to use public tools safely for their role.

## **9\. Differences by APS/EL level**

*\[Figure missing: mitigations\_by\_level.png\]*

There were some differences by APS/EL level. EL users reported higher security comfort than APS users, while APS users rated the introductory email and splash screens more positively. This may suggest that EL users were more confident applying judgement, while APS users may have relied more on structured communications and point-of-use reminders. Because subgroup sample sizes are small, this finding should be framed as directional rather than definitive.

## **Recommended wording for the report**

Overall, the trial mitigations were effective enough to support a controlled trial, but not sufficient as a standalone model for broader rollout. The strongest evidence is that safety communications and splash screens helped create informed caution: around seven in ten valid users rated each as moderately or highly effective, and users who rated both positively were more likely to feel comfortable using public tools. Reported security concerns were rare, and no valid user rated upload blockers as ineffective. However, the evidence also shows limits. Most users did not notice upload blockers, meaning the survey provides limited direct evidence of DLP performance. Copying and pasting information was more common than document upload, which means the main residual risk depends heavily on user judgement rather than technical blocking. Non-user responses also show that security concerns and uncertainty about appropriate use remain barriers. Future rollout should therefore keep DLP controls, retain point-of-use reminders, and strengthen practical training on safe prompting, copy/paste behaviour, document classification and judgement calls.

## **Implications for future rollout**

* Retain DLP upload blockers, but treat them as a backstop rather than the whole mitigation strategy.  
* Use system logs to supplement survey evidence on whether DLP controls were triggered and whether attempted uploads were blocked.  
* Retain splash screens, but periodically refresh wording to avoid staff becoming habituated to the warning.  
* Strengthen training with practical, role-specific scenarios showing what can and cannot be shared.  
* Add specific guidance on copy/paste behaviour, not just document upload.  
* Provide examples for common grey areas, including project artefacts, commercially sensitive information, unclear document markings, internal emails, meeting notes, code and data extracts.  
* Clarify that human review remains required, especially for sensitive policy, legal, ethical or public-facing outputs.  
* Consider targeted support for staff who did not use the tools because of security concerns or uncertainty about use cases.

## **Limitations**

* The analysis is based on self-reported survey data and does not verify actual DLP events.  
* The upload blocker item is difficult to interpret because users would only notice blockers if they attempted an upload that triggered the control.  
* There was no direct survey item asking whether formal training was effective. Training effectiveness is inferred from safety communication ratings, confidence, non-user reasons and reported capability uplift.  
* Subgroup findings by APS/EL level should be treated as directional due to small sample sizes.  
* The trial used free public tools and took place over a short period, so results may not generalise to longer-term use or enterprise-tier products.
